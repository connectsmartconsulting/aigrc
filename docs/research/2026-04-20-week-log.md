# R&D log: week of 2026-04-20

**Author:** Safiuddin Mohammed Ahmed
**Project:** aigrc v0.1

## What we were trying to learn

Whether an executable check runner organised around regulatory framework mappings (NIST AI RMF MEASURE, EU AI Act Article 15, ISO 42001 Annex A, OWASP LLM Top 10) could produce audit-grade evidence from a single payload class (prompt injection), and what the right shape of such a tool is for integration into client CI pipelines.

## What we tried

1. Surveyed existing open-source options (Garak, PyRIT, Promptfoo) for how they structure payloads, evaluators, and reports. None of them organise around regulatory framework controls. All three treat evaluation and execution as a single concern.
2. Prototyped two different evaluator patterns: an LLM-as-judge approach and a deterministic heuristic approach.
3. Drafted YAML mappings for all four framework catalogues and tested whether "coverage status" (live, planned, out of scope) was granular enough to produce honest claims about what aigrc actually validates today.

## What we learned

1. The regulatory mapping is the differentiator. Red-team tools produce payload pass/fail counts. Audit-ready evidence requires control-level traceability. Putting the mappings in YAML and referencing them by control ID inside each check module produces reports that an auditor can follow without learning the tool.
2. Deterministic heuristics beat LLM-judge for v0.1 (see ADR-0001). The main cost is false positive rate on refusal detection, which we mitigated with a two-signature check (system prompt leak requires two distinctive substrings, not one).
3. An offline mock target is non-negotiable. Without it, CI runs are expensive, demos are brittle, and design-partner conversations stall on setup friction. The mock should produce a realistic partial-compliance result (not 100% or 0%) to be credible.
4. The 18-payload taxonomy converged on OWASP LLM01:2025 categories plus techniques from Perez and Ribeiro 2022 and Greshake et al. 2023. This is the right coverage for v0.1.

## Uncertainty resolved

- **Uncertainty:** How granular should regulatory mappings be? **Resolved:** Control-ID level (NIST MEASURE 2.7, not NIST MEASURE). Anything coarser produces vague reports. Anything finer produces maintenance burden without evidentiary value.
- **Uncertainty:** Should we use a single-concern check module or split payloads and evaluators into separate files? **Resolved:** Single-concern. Each check is one payload family plus its evaluation logic in one file. This makes each check independently auditable and reduces cross-cutting edits.

## Uncertainty still open

- **Open:** Optimal heuristic for detecting successful jailbreak across language switches (payload 014). Current heuristic catches most cases but has elevated false-positive rate when the model responds in the non-English language legitimately. Deferred to v0.2.
- **Open:** Whether reports should include a suggested remediation field per finding, or whether remediation belongs exclusively in Qopilot output. Leaning toward Qopilot-only to preserve the separation of concerns.
